#### Martin Heidegger | Philosophy

Martin Heidegger was a German philosopher who penned on the topics of ontology, epistemology, ethics, and politics. As one of the leading figures in existentialism, this article will give you an overview of his life and work while also helping to explain some key terms found within his writings. We'll also explore modern interpretations of his ideas.

Martin Heidegger was born back on 26 September 1889 right here in Messkirch Germany. Martin's dad used to be a Catholic priest and his mom, also a strong believer, was the one in charge of raising him with his elder brother Karl.

He had quite fond memories from his younger days when he would go out playing soccer regularly along with all of his friends, but by adulthood that interest just withered away. Could it have been something else than natural maturation? What made Martin drift away from sports so quickly?

Instead of emphasizing sports, Heidegger developed an interest in philosophy and theology very early — so much so that when it was time for him to go to university (at 19), he opted for the study of these disciplines rather than going after medicine or law like many youths at that period.

> **=="For words and language are not wrappings in which things are packed for the commerce of those who write and speak. It is in words and language that things first come into being and are."**
> **― Martin Heidegger, Introduction to Metaphysics==**

Heidegger studied theology and philosophy at the University of Freiburg where his tutors included Edmund Husserl who eventually became one his greatest influences along with a mentor while they worked together from 1919–1923\. Could this be why their works have remained relevant to date?

Heidegger eventually earned his doctorate degree from Freiburg after writing two dissertations: "The Doctrine Of Being In The Grundgesetze Der Metaphysik" (1916) which focused on German idealism and "On The Essence Of Truth" (1918) that delved into Friedrich Nietzsche's ideas about truthfulness versus lying.

### Heidegger's Critique of Metaphysics

His critique of regular metaphysics was not constrained to the ontological contrast. He contended that any attempt to comprehend Existence, or "the idea of reality," must consider what he called its temporal nature.

He argued this because time is essential for us to experience anything; it shapes our comprehension in a way nothing else can. Time allows us questions such as 'What came first?

> **"Everywhere we remain unfree and chained to technology, whether we passionately affirm or deny it. But we are delivered over to it in the worst possible way when we regard it as something neutral; for this conception of it, to which today we particularly pay homage, makes us utterly blind to the essence of technology." ― Martin Heidegger**

What happened next?' And consequently helps give structure and significance to everything we encounter — individually or collectively — throughout life.

When it comes to humans, who exist in time and are only able to experience reality through their own experiences, we cannot get an unbiased view of things independent from our perceptions and beliefs; thus every effort at figuring out the truth needs to take into account this restriction.

This concept has profound impacts on how we understand knowledge: If we can't step away from ourselves in order to gain access to objective facts about what's going on around us (as Descartes tried), then how do we know anything?

The answer lies in Heidegger's concept of being itself — embedded within us before we even consider thinking about anything else!

### Dasein: Heidegger's Idea of Existing-in-the World

'Dasein' is a German term meaning 'being.' For Martin Heidegger, it denotes human beings as entities that exist within the world.

This implies we can't completely grasp ourselves or our experiences without understanding how we link with other folks and stuff around us.

In short, Dasein reveals us to be interconnected parts in a larger jigsaw puzzle — all being part and parcel of something much bigger than just ourselves. What does this mean for each one of us? Does living life from this perspective allow insight into who we are at our core?

> **"For Heidegger, boredom is a privileged fundamental mood because it leads us directly into the very problem complex of being and time." ― Lars Fr. H. Svendsen, A Philosophy of Boredom**

In his book **Being and Time** (1927), Heidegger put forward the idea that humans have no choice over aspects such as when they are born or where they come from — we're just "thrown" into existence.

On the other hand, our awareness of mortality means that each one of us is also able to reflect on which paths in life we should take and how best to live — it's like a projection into future possibilities. How might this realization shape your decisions?

### Heidegger's Idea of Authenticity

The idea of genuineness is a major part of Heidegger's thinking. In fact, it's one of the few ideas that he applies regularly in his writings.

Combining these two aspects creates an active process for each person to explore different roles within various circles while trying to find out which path can bring delight or gratification with those occupations; this provides rise not just to individual searches but also cultural ones like understanding significance in artworks manufactured by other people before us.

Have you ever asked yourself whether there is real joy and fulfillment beyond material wealth? What would be your answer if someone were to ask what genuine contentment means from your life perspective?

> **"Turning away from a flight from death, you see a horizon of opportunity that puts you in a state of anticipatory resoluteness with solicitous regard for others that makes your life seem like an adventure perfused with unshakeable joy." ― Heidegger, Martin**

These questions I guess might lead us closer to grasping Heidegger's concept of authenticity — attempting an honest portrayal of ourselves through our choices and actions no matter how difficult they could be sometimes.

It's a concept rooted in the philosophy of Martin Heidegger, who described authenticity as an individual's ability to create meaning from their own life experience. He argued that we all have our own unique paths and it is up to us to make sense of them.

In order for someone to be truly authentic, they must accept themselves fully — the good and bad parts. This means being honest about your feelings and opinions even when they may not be popular or accepted by others.

Authenticity also requires one to take responsibility for their actions; if you do something wrong then don't try to blame anyone else but instead face up the consequences with courage."

It's important to get familiar with what he implies by using the word "authentic" plus how this deduction associates itself alongside other theories inside ideology before starting pondering over its usage within personal lives Eigentlich (pronounced "EYE-gent-ly") which is the German equivalent of English term "Authentic" pretty much translates into "…appropriately belonging".

For further elaboration on the same topic philosopher Martin Heidegger gave his opinion where he describes authentiсity as the human capacity towards creating a meaningful existence derived from prior living experiences administrations.

According to him, everybody has different routes throughout their lifespan so the main task remains upon oneself to invent valid explanations behind these occurrences

If desires obtain the level of true authenticity one needs to comprehend both the benefits & drawbacks side since honesty plays a great role here Specifically speaking expressing concerns (&opinions ) no matter whether such statements are well accepted among the crowd acceptable, Not only the show high spirit however accompanies along commitment taking full responsibly either regarding negative or positive aftermaths besides standing forth fearlessly facing subsequent outcomes

When we talk about something being authentic, what do we mean? In simple terms, it means that the item or idea belongs where it is and nowhere else.

Let's say for example you were born in Boston but moved away when you were three years old to live with your family in New York City. Then, even though those two places are yours technically speaking — they don't really have any influence on who you truly are; therefore neither one of them can be considered an authentic part of yourself!

So when considering authenticity- all things must fit into their proper place without exception; otherwise, they simply become irrelevant parts of our lives that cannot contribute anything meaningful to us.

In short: Whenever trying to determine if something is authentically ours- ask yourself a question 'Does this belong here?' If not then most likely there's no point in categorizing it as so.

This concept can also be expanded: if someone loves sports but has a distaste for reading books, then they might consider their enthusiasm for athletics to reflect who they truly are since that is the activity with which they spend more time.

However, when it comes to another person whose interests span both books and football games; neither of these interests would necessarily define them (although elements from either interest could come close).

### Heidegger's Impact On Phenomenology

Heidegger made considerable contributions to what would become known as phenomenology–a form of philosophy rooted in reflection and inquiry about existence itself rather than relying solely on logical argumentation or rigid ideas from traditional metaphysics (i.e., their significance).

One major element that came out of his writings was "phenomenological reduction" — not a direct route but still offering insight into our relationships between self-and-environment; something thinkers had sought for centuries prior yet had failed at resolving until then.

> **"Heidegger's concept for the kind of being we ourselves are is Dasein. Literally it means 'being-there'.We are the sort of beings who are there, in the world. What characterizes Dasein is that its existence is a concern for it in its existence."**

It's safe to say that Heidegger left quite a mark when it comes to this branch of philosophy, providing much-needed perspective related specifically to people's interactions with their surroundings — can such careful consideration really help someone gain greater knowledge about themselves while learning more truths beyond? It appears so!

Heidegger's impact on phenomenology was tremendous and it helped shape what we know as the practice today. His contributions were substantial, for instance:

The idea is that philosophical inquiry should be grounded in experience rather than logic or reason only.

A notion that our self-understanding is attained by understanding how we interact with other entities (e.g., people, trees, or cars) surrounding us. An emphasis on Dasein being part of a bigger context instead of just human consciousness alone.
